Mental health conditions that arise after experiencing or witnessing trauma ‚Äî such as accidents, violence, loss, or disasters.

Overview

These disorders occur following a stressful or traumatic event and were grouped together in the DSM-5 because they share a clear link to trauma or stress. Common examples include:

  • Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD): Develops after exposure to severe trauma such as assault, war, or disaster.
  • Acute Stress Disorder (ASD): Similar to PTSD, but symptoms occur immediately after trauma and last less than one month.
  • Adjustment Disorders (AD): Emotional or behavioral changes that occur in response to a major life stressor such as loss, breakup, or relocation.

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Symptoms

Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)

  • Flashbacks or vivid, distressing memories of the trauma
  • Nightmares and disturbed sleep
  • Avoidance of trauma reminders
  • Feeling emotionally numb or detached
  • Hypervigilance or being easily startled
  • Negative thoughts about self, others, or the world

Acute Stress Disorder (ASD)

  • Similar symptoms as PTSD (flashbacks, avoidance, anxiety)
  • Develops within days of trauma and lasts from 3 days to 1 month
  • If symptoms persist longer, it may develop into PTSD

Adjustment Disorder (AD)

  • Sadness, hopelessness, or excessive worry after a stressor
  • Difficulty functioning at work, school, or socially
  • Physical symptoms such as headaches or stomach pain
  • Usually starts within 3 months of a major life event

üí¨ Causes and Risk Factors

  • Severe or repeated trauma: War, abuse, accidents, or disasters
  • Loss or major life changes: Death of a loved one, relationship breakup, job loss
  • Childhood adversity: Neglect, violence, or unstable environments
  • Lack of social support: Isolation increases vulnerability
  • Biological factors: Overactive stress response systems or genetic susceptibility

üå± How to Overcome or Manage

1. Professional Support

  • Therapy:
    • Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT): Helps reframe negative thought patterns linked to trauma.
    • Exposure Therapy: Safely confronts trauma reminders to reduce avoidance.
    • Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing (EMDR): Effective for PTSD symptom reduction.
  • Medication: Antidepressants (SSRIs), anti-anxiety medicines, or sleep aids as prescribed by a psychiatrist.

2. Self-Help & Lifestyle

  • Practice mindfulness and grounding techniques (deep breathing, sensory focus).
  • Follow a consistent routine ‚Äî balanced meals, adequate rest, and light exercise.
  • Avoid alcohol, drugs, or overuse of screens to cope.
  • Stay socially connected with supportive friends, family, or community groups.
  • Express emotions through journaling, art, or creative outlets.

3. Early Intervention

  • Seek support soon after a traumatic event to prevent long-term distress.
  • If symptoms worsen or involve suicidal thoughts, contact helplines or emergency care immediately.
